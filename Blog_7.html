A reflection on my interactive data driven artwork

In this blog post, I will reflect on the interactive data-driven artwork I have created using D3. By discussing the data-driven artwork I have created, the interactivity, and the creation of the meaning of my artwork. As well as relating it to Kidd (2015) and his theory of representation.
When it comes to understanding large amounts of data and statistics, data visualization is a very useful and important tool we are able to utilize in order to create meaning and understanding of the data represented. There are many ways that we are able to represent data visually from charts to graphs, to interactive data sets, the main point of creating these visualizations is to take the bland straight forward data and depict it in a new and interesting way that provides readability and meaning to the person viewing the data.

In creating my exam data-driven artwork, the data I wanted to represent focuses on the difference in population in South African provinces from 2019 to 2020 in relation to the population who are currently infected with the Corona Virus. In order to visually represent this data, I created a bubble map whereby I overlap the bubbles of data depicting the population size in 2019, 2020, and the currently infected population for each province. The reason I chose to visualize my data in this way is that by overlapping the bubbles it shows a clear indication of the comparison between the years as well as how much of our population is currently infected with the Corona Virus. Then to further enhance the data I created interactivity for the viewer of my visualization, by adding checkboxes that the user can tick on and off to display the data, this interactivity allows the user to see for themselves the comparison between the bubbles of data.



I am happy with my data-driven artwork as I believe it puts into perspective the total population of people who currently are infected with the Corona Virus, in comparison to the population size. When looking at the comparison it shows how small the population of infected is to the population size, providing hope that we can overcome this virus. However, due to the virus being so contagious even if there is such a small percentage of infected people it can still spread easily and quickly. Thus, the created meaning of my data aims to show that if we all follow the right procedures of wearing a mask, sanitizing and washing our hands, and maintain social distancing we can counteract the numbers and reduce the spread of the virus. This is the kind of visual communication I am trying to portray with my data, however, since visual communication relies so heavily on the viewer's interpretation of the data it is impossible for everyone who views my given data to deduce the same meaning. Therefore even though my hopes are for the people who view my data are to understand that we have a fighting chance against the virus if we act with caution, that does not mean that everyone will derive the same meaning.
“Visual communication can be understood as more ambiguous, suggestive, and open to multiple interpretations than other forms of communication, not least because the codes that are utilized remain unspoken, are not pinned down in language, and are nontextual.” (Kidd. J, 2015)
Therefore, in relation to Kidd’s discussion on theories of representation, I have learned when creating my data-driven artwork that the data being represented can be understood in many different ways to each person. In terms of my data, the representation I am trying to show may not come across the same to another person who has had a different experience of the pandemic. Thus, meaning created through my data will vary between people due to different cultures, experiences, and understandings.
“We are reminded again that representations are constructive; they frame and feed into our understandings of the world and our place within it.” (Kidd. J, 2015)
Overall, I am happy with the data I have represented. However, In order to improve on this data representation, I would have liked to be able to add a tooltip that when the viewer's mouse hovers over each of the bubbles it would provide them with the name and the number of data being represented in that bubble. As well as being able to label each of the provinces in order to clearly depict the data.
References:
